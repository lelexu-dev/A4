Why does the Strait of Hormuz matter so much to the global energy supply?

By Lele Xu March 2026

Rising geopolitical tensions in the Middle East have once again drawn global attention to a narrow stretch of water between Iran and Oman: the Strait of Hormuz. Although it appears small on the map, the strait plays an outsized role in the global economy. A large share of the world’s oil and natural gas exports must pass through this corridor before reaching international markets.

Because of this geographic bottleneck, even the possibility of disruptions to shipping in the strait can affect global energy prices. Governments, energy traders and analysts closely monitor developments in the region, knowing that instability here can quickly ripple across global supply chains.

Source: Natural Earth | Graphic: Lele Xu

The Strait of Hormuz serves as the only maritime outlet for several major oil-producing countries in the Persian Gulf, including Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates. Tankers carrying crude oil and liquefied natural gas must travel through the strait before reaching markets in Asia, Europe and beyond.

According to the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA), roughly 20 million barrels of oil pass through the Strait of Hormuz each day, representing around 20–30% of global seaborne oil trade. This makes it the most important energy transit chokepoint in the world.

Source: UNCTAD, Clarksons Research

Energy shipments dominate maritime traffic in the Strait of Hormuz, highlighting how heavily global energy markets depend on this route. Oil and gas transported from Persian Gulf producers supply many major importing economies around the world.

Asian countries in particular rely heavily on energy shipments passing through the strait. China, Japan, India and South Korea import large volumes of Middle Eastern oil to support industrial production and transportation systems. Because these economies depend on stable energy supplies, any disruption to shipping routes in the region could quickly affect global energy markets.

The data show that a significant share of oil exports from countries such as Saudi Arabia, Iraq and the United Arab Emirates ultimately flow toward Asian markets.

Another factor that makes the Strait of Hormuz strategically significant is its geography. At its narrowest point, the waterway is only about 33 kilometers wide, and parts of the channel lie within Iranian territorial waters. This makes the passage vulnerable to geopolitical tensions and military conflict.

Because so much of the world’s energy supply passes through this corridor, even temporary disruptions can quickly affect energy markets. Oil traders closely monitor developments in the region as potential threats to tanker traffic.

“The only way to see oil prices trade lower on a sustained basis is by getting oil flowing through the Strait of Hormuz,” analysts at ING said in a recent market report. “Failing to do so means that the market highs are still ahead of us.”

The comment reflects how closely global oil prices are tied to shipping flows through the strait.

Despite its small size on the map, the Strait of Hormuz functions as a critical artery of the global energy system. Oil and gas exports from the Persian Gulf depend heavily on this narrow shipping corridor, and few viable alternatives exist that could fully replace its capacity.

Understanding the scale of energy flows through the strait helps explain why policymakers, shipping companies and financial markets react quickly to developments in the region. As long as global energy supply chains remain concentrated around this chokepoint, the Strait of Hormuz will continue to shape global trade and energy security.
